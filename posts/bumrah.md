174 freakish, insane, and magical deliveries to win a World Cup

*Disclaimer:*

Jasprit Bumrah has bowled 174 balls in the T20 World Cup 2026 (including
extras) and has given away 174 runs. On average he gave away 6 runs per
over. The previous T20 world cup in the Carribean islands and USA, was a
bowler-friendly tournament, but not this one. The average economy for
the entire tournament was 8.58 runs per over and in the non-Sri Lanka
matches, it was 9.08. Essentially, all the other bowlers were setting up
180 run games, while Bumrah was playing a 120-run game.

That is *insane.*

Most batsmen were looking to play out his overs safely, knock the ball
around, and *yet* he ended up being the highest wicket taker---14 at
1.75 wickets a game. And anyone who saw him bowl in 2023-24 will feel
that he is only at about 80% of his peak form.

That is *freakishly insane.*

Enough has been written about the greatness of Bumrah, his god-like
performances and his undeniable impact on a cricket match. I am only
writing as a fan, an amateur follower of the sport and a novice numbers
cruncher of the game. I think future historians must have enough pieces
of unconnected information about this legend to make sure he is never
forgotten.

Let me start of by saying, I have been watching cricket since the age of
5, since 2005, for more than 20 years, and I am sure, absolutely sure,
that in the past 20 years no cricketer has come close to this
superhuman. Look, I am not a great believer in the numbers game many
fans get into, but the stats can be useful in defining the kind of freak
show Jasprit Bumrah has put up in this world cup. Watching him bowl is a
privilege in itself, but I wanted to define---in a few meagre ways---how
well he has bowled; again, stating that he was not his full self in this
tournament. His aura, which immediately ensures that the batsman go on
the defensive, however fully makes up for any lack in form or fitness.

Like I said before, Bumrah has an economy of 6 and also takes a wicket
every 12 balls while giving away 12 runs in between taking wickets. If I
had to oversimplify those numbers (not to dumb them down, but to sound
more poetic):

*If Bumrah bowled **all** 20 overs, he would bowl the **team out** in 20
overs giving away **just** 120 runs.*

Sometimes, I wonder if the above statement is even an exaggeration. He
could---in an alternate universe---tirelessly bowl 20 overs and, in
fact, bowl out teams for 120. And yes, I am considering that, in such a
wild situation, teams cannot play him out and will try to attack him; to
which I say, bring it on. That would only make him more lethal. He is
just that good in my books.

Going back to the numbers, let us look at the Indian bowling attack with
Bumrah in it: They give 8.5 runs on average every over, take a wicket
every 16 balls and give away 22.5 runs per wicket. That is a very good
T20 bowling attack if you think about it; they would take around 7-8
wickets and give away 170 runs in the process. Given the deep batting
attack, which hits 10-20 runs above par regularly, hitting 200 and
defending the score with 10-20 runs to spare seems very fair.

But now, let us remove Bumrah from the attack. Now, the rest of the
bowlers give away 9.1 runs per average, takes a wicket every 17 balls
and give away 25.5 runs on average. This implies they would take 7
wickets and give away around 180 runs in the process. You might wonder
why I am making a huge fuss of these numbers; at the end of the day,
they only take 1 wicket lesser and give away around 10 runs more. Think
again.

*To be precise, without Bumrah, the team is likely to give away 11 extra
runs every match. To be pedantic, I am assuming the hypothetical bowler
that replaces Bumrah is an entity who performs at par with the rest of
the bowling line-up; he has neither bad days nor good days, just a
middling performer who gives away exactly as much as the average of his
teammates.*

A margin of 11 runs was the difference between losing and winning in 3
out of 8 matches India played and would have been an even more hurting
defeat against South Africa. The obvious example is the England match,
in which the English lose by 6 runs. Granted Archer smashed a few sixes
against Dube who was trying to just bowl six legal deliveries. But
consider the 19^th^ over Hardik bowled; at the start of the over England
needed 39 off 12 balls. Subtract 11 runs from it, now they need 28 runs
off 12. Do you think Hardik Pandya (a bonafide legend in his own right)
and Shivam Dube would have defended 28 off 12 against the fire-breathing
centurion Jacob Bethell?

Take the West Indies match now. WI scored 194 and India chased it down
with 4 balls to spare, courtesy an otherworldly Sanju Samson knock.
Anyone watching that match must have felt the pressure when a fresh Dube
was facing Shamar Joseph, requiring 17 to get of 10 balls. Now think
Dube requires 28 to get off 10 balls.... In fact, Netherlands lost by 17
runs, and if we consider these 11 runs, they were one hit away from a
win.

We can all agree on the fact that these are hypothetical situations, and
cricket is a glorious game of uncertainties. India is such a good T20
team currently, that they would have had answers and an extra gear for
these moments---but hand on your heart---in the situations I have
painted above, don't you think India's chances of winning worsened quite
a bit?

And that is only a naïve numbers-based assessment of Bumrah's impact.
For me personally, the real impact is the following situation I have
found myself in so many times in the past few years. The game is on a
knife-edge, runs are leaking, a batsman is smashing it all around. All
the other bowlers are being taken to the cleaners. We need something to
happen; in fact we need that one batsman to get out. Not the other
batsman playing second fiddle, not a quiet over which goes just below
average. We need what is called a shut-down over. And 9 times out of 10
when I have felt we needed that swing in the game, Bumrah is given the
ball, and Bumrah strikes.

And this is uncanny, this isn't a one-off occurrence, it isn't a fluke.
And the best part; most batsmen know that they shouldn't give their
wicket away to him. They are extra cautious. They are playing him out,
and *yet* he takes a wicket.

As a fan, I must say here that Bumrah keeps answering our trust time and
again and we must never get used to that feeling. That trust in him that
he keeps responding to might never happen again in our life with another
cricketer, and that feeling is something we must cherish for a lifetime.

I have seen enough IPL games to see enough bowlers take the least
impactful wickets in the death overs, while Bumrah takes the most
important wickets almost always. England's best batsman was Harry Brook,
and Bumrah takes that wicket. West Indies' best batsman was Shimron
Hetmeyer and he takes that wicket. Their second-best batsman on that day
was Roston Chase, and he takes that wicket too. Pakistan were reeling
after losing their best batsman to Hardik; do they think they can wake
up and make a fist of the chase? No, because Bumrah bowls inswinging
yorkers in the second over and leaves them at 13/3. All his wickets
either stem the flow of runs or completely kills the opposition, so the
11 runs lesser India concede because of him is just the beginning. In
fact, every opposing team knows that they are going to get 11 runs
lesser off him, and so they go harder at the other bowlers. Sometimes it
comes off, and other times he leads to his teammates getting wickets off
the pressure he built up on his end. Well, he does mop up the tail too,
like we saw in the final, and he deserves to get those "easy" wickets at
the end. The only game he didn't take a wicket was against Zimbabwe who
were chasing 256 but forgot it was a T20 and it was not an ODI. No
seriously, they were aiming to get 180 on a batting paradise. In that
match he conceded 21 runs, out of which he got hit for 6 to a bouncer
(one of the few clean sixes he gave away).

In fact, that is the next thing to look at, out of the 174 balls he
bowled, he gave only 20 boundary balls, out of which 4 were sixes. I
mentioned the Zimbabwe six (I think his bouncer has not been the best
this tournament), Saim Ayub's six was a complete edge (no need for
documentaries on that one), Jason holder smashed one in the last over of
the match (an actual mis-executed yorker by Bumrah, worth recording for
posteriority), and Bethell whipped a wayward ball (seeing that he played
an absolute beast of a knock we can excuse that six). In and of itself,
the fact that I can list down the exact situations a six was hit of him
is *insane*. You will see most pundits of the game were *worried* that
he gave as many as a grand total of 4 sixes in 28 overs. **That** is the
legend of Bumrah.

I think we all know by now that Bumrah's success comes from his
hyperextension, excellent wrists and an accuracy that hits the right
length all the time. A bowler usually has three variables to control in
his arsenal (generally speaking, swing and seam movement is condition
dependent). Line, length and pace. What I find delightful to watch in
Bumrah's bowling is that he has a special ball to bowl with every
combination of those three variables; notwithstanding the backspin and
dip he generates on the ball. His go-to bowl is a clean delivery that
hits the good length and aim at the middle and off stumps. That ball
itself is quite hard to get away. But in T20 you need more variations
than that to be able to defend and attack when required. And in the 174
balls he bowled in this world cup, he showed us every bit of those
variations. As far as **I** noticed and correlated with online
commentary, he bowled 79 out of 174 length balls of which 26 would be
back-of-length, 26 would be length, and 26 would be slower variations on
a good length. He bowled 31 fullish deliveries of varying lines, with
some dip and not always in the slot. And then, to top it all of he
bowled 30 yorkers; inswinging, dipping, slow and whatnot. He missed 12
yorkers to give away ankle-height full tosses, but his full tosses are
so good he conceded only 17 runs from them. That is *freakish* accuracy.
The rest were bouncers, slow and quick mixed together. To put it simply,
take Malinga's yorker, Bravo's slower dipping balls, Hazlewood's
accuracy and good length, Archer's sharp bouncer, Boult/Starc's swinging
deliveries. Put them all together and take away about 25% of their
potency (so that I am being fair to the others), and you have Jasprit
Bumrah.

He deceived 3 batsmen with his cunning slow balls in 3 consecutive
knockout matches. One plumb lbw with an inswinger, 5 wickets to yorkers.
Took Hetmeyer's wicket with a cramped up shortish delivery, which was
pitch perfect execution to Shimron's weakness, and the rest of his
wickets fell with his stock back of length delivery. He was probably
gifted around 3 out of his 14 wickets by poor shot selection. *The rest
were just not playable.* As a final stat, he had figures of 4/15, 3/15
and 1/33 against NZ, SA and England; the three other semi-finalists.

India has won the 2026 T20 world cup, defending its title from 2024 and
I am quite sure that we would have not won either of them without
Jasprit Singh Bumrah. We would have not won this world cup if not for
these freakish, insane, and magical 174 deliveries...
